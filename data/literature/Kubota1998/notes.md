## Why this paper

Originally identified as the companion-of-record to `Kondo1998`. On full extraction the proceedings paper turns out to be the more comprehensive of the two: it pairs the CW 266 nm reliability number with (i) a structural Cz-BBO characterisation (boule, polish, linear absorption, Rayleigh scattering, thermal conductivity, two-photon absorption coefficient), (ii) a unified damage-probability model `p(E) = C exp(-K/E)` fitted independently in the CW and pulsed regimes, and (iii) a quantitative connection between the CW and pulsed damage rates that the Letter does not provide. For Task E it is the bibliographic anchor for both `KD-UV280-005` (Q1 / Q2 numerical) and `KD-UV280-011` (Q3 mechanism: thermal-excitation → multiphoton-excitation transition).

## Extraction Scope

The 2026-05-06 pass extracts the full body of the five-page proceedings paper across the introduction, the §"CW-266 nm generation in BBO" section, the §"Pulsed 266 nm generation in BBO" section, the discussion, and the conclusion. Specifically:

- Cz-BBO crystal growth (boule dimensions, growth time, surface roughness, defect-density advantage over flux-grown BBO).
- Linear-absorption-edge structure (213 nm absorption coefficient in e-polarisation, exponential tail terminating ~230 nm, attributed to LO phonon).
- Long-wavelength scattering loss (2 % / cm o-pol, 1 % / cm e-pol at 266 nm; Rayleigh-scaling 1/λ⁴ behaviour).
- Two-photon absorption coefficient at 266 nm.
- Thermal conductivity (anisotropic, parallel and perpendicular to c-axis).
- The CW 266 nm generation parameters (cavity reflectance, enhancement factor, intracavity power, single-pass nonlinear gain, round-trip cavity loss).
- The CW reliability result: 5 × 10⁻⁵ % / h cavity-loss-increase rate at 260 W / cm² average UV power density on three samples; lifetime extrapolation 2000 h to 0.1 % loss increase under input-power compensation.
- The pulsed 266 nm reliability result at 7 kHz / 29 ns / 31 MW / cm² peak: 0.05 % / h transmittance-decrease rate, 0.8 W average UV from 2.6 W average green at 31 % conversion, single-pulse damage probability p ≈ 2 × 10⁻¹¹.
- The empirical damage-probability model `p(E) = C exp(-K/E)` with separate CW and pulsed parameter sets.
- The discussion of the C₂/C₁ ratio of order 10⁻⁶ as evidence of a thermal-excitation → multiphoton-excitation transition in the electron-supply mechanism behind local avalanche optical breakdown.

The extraction is a Phase 1 literature artefact only. The same wavelength-mapping caveat from `Kondo1998` applies: this is a 266 nm and 213 nm paper, not a 280 nm paper. The headline numbers must not be ported to `KD-UV280-005 Section C` without an explicit search-boundary statement and a phase-matching-angle / d_eff conversion.

## Review Notes

- **Programme overlap with Kondo1998 is confirmed.** Authorship overlap, same Sony Research Center affiliation, same operating point (532 → 266 nm SHG, 100 mW UV from 500 mW green, w₀ = 46 μm, three samples, 1000+ h, 0.4 L/min N₂ purge), identical d_eff = 1.64 pm/V, and matching γ_SH = 5 × 10⁻⁵ W⁻¹ all confirm this is the same experimental run reported in two venues. The proceedings paper extends the Letter with the crystal characterisation, the pulsed result, and the damage model. **For Section C use, treat Kondo1998 + Kubota1998 as one programme**, not two independent measurements.
- **Geometry vs Kondo1998: a small discrepancy.** Kondo1998 reports the BBO at 47.5° cut and ρ = 4.88°. Kubota1998 reports the SHG phase-matching and walk-off angles as 47.9° and 4.8°, computed from the Sellmeier equation for Cz-BBO of ref [5] (Kato 1990). The difference is consistent with cut-vs-phase-matching distinction (cut angle is a fabrication parameter; phase-matching angle is a Sellmeier prediction at the operating temperature). No flag needed beyond the `phase_match_vs_cut_angle` cross-check below.
- **Walk-off-parameter convention difference.** Kondo1998 reports the walk-off parameter as `B = ρ √(kl) / 2 = 14.67`. Kubota1998 reports `β = 37.9` for the same setup. The two values are not numerically consistent under the Kondo definition; they reflect different walk-off-parameter conventions in the Boyd-Kleinman literature. The single-pass conversion factor `γ_SH = 5 × 10⁻⁵ W⁻¹` is consistent between papers, so the underlying physics agrees; only the dimensionless walk-off label differs. Both values are recorded as-stated and flagged in `cross_check_notes`.
- **CW round-trip-loss decomposition.** The proceedings paper decomposes the 0.75 % initial round-trip loss as: 0.3 % from the four high-reflectivity mirrors + ~0.036 % Rayleigh scattering (computed from Sellmeier-derived index for o-ray at 532 nm in 0.6-cm BBO) + smaller contributions from absorption and bulk scattering. This decomposition is a useful orthogonal check on the overall mirror loss budget when comparing to Friedenauer 2006's Hänsch-Couillaud cavity.
- **CW vs pulsed damage rate gap.** The CW degradation rate is `5 × 10⁻⁵ % / h` at 260 W / cm² and the pulsed degradation rate is `0.05 % / h` at 31 MW / cm² peak. The 1000× rate ratio for ~10⁵× higher peak power density is consistent with a strongly nonlinear damage-probability scaling, which the authors model as the `exp(-K/E)` form in §6.
- **Damage-probability model is the most novel contribution.** Eqs. 6-7 fit `p(E) = C exp(-K/E)` separately to the CW and pulsed regimes. The CW parameters (C₂ = 2.1 × 10⁻¹⁷, K₂ = 50 kV / mm corresponding to 550 W / cm² in the converted units) and the pulsed parameters (C₁ = 3.5-6.5 × 10⁻¹¹, K₁ = 7 MV / m corresponding to 11 MW / cm²) differ by ~10⁶ in C and ~140 in K. The authors interpret this as evidence of a transition from thermal excitation to multiphoton excitation as the dominant electron-supply mechanism behind local avalanche optical breakdown. **For `KD-UV280-011` this is the most directly transferable published mechanism statement**, but the wavelength caveat still applies.
- **Microdefect localisation evidence.** Fig. 5 (a HeNe tomographic image after pulsed 266 nm operation) shows defect formation along the *generated UV* propagation direction, not along the input 532 nm direction. The authors conclude damage is induced primarily by the 266 nm UV, not the green pump. This rules out the green-pump as the dominant degradation actor and is the cleanest single-paragraph evidence in the literature for UV-driven (not green-driven) BBO surface failure.
- **Linear absorption tail, 2PA, and thermal conductivity.** These three numbers are not headline reliability evidence but are the building blocks of any thermal-load model for BBO at the UV stage. They should populate the BBO row of the §5 Phase 1 evidence table in the dossier independent of the >1000 h reliability claim.
- **Sample-vendor metadata still incomplete.** The paper makes clear the BBO was Czochralski-grown by the Sony team itself (not purchased from a vendor), and references their own work (refs [4], [5]) on the growth process. The "three samples" referred to in this paper and in Kondo1998 are therefore Sony-internal Cz-BBO. This means neither paper provides direct evidence about Crystals of Siberia, CASTECH, EKSMA, or FEEFO BBO — the vendor-comparison TODO in `KD-UV280-005 Section C` is not closed by Kondo1998 + Kubota1998.

## Downstream Dossier Links

- `KD-UV280-005`: BBO at 280 nm — phase-matching, walk-off, damage threshold. Kubota1998 is the bibliographic anchor for the CW operational-lifetime number (5 × 10⁻⁵ % / h at 260 W / cm² average UV power density at 266 nm, three Sony Cz-BBO samples) and supplies the linear-absorption, Rayleigh-scattering, two-photon-absorption, and thermal-conductivity numbers for the §5 Phase 1 evidence table. Adjacent-wavelength caveat applies.
- `KD-UV280-009`: BBO surface degradation and gas-environment dependence. Kubota1998 corroborates the dry-N₂ purge protocol in Kondo1998 and supplies a hygroscopicity statement ("BBO is slightly hygroscopic; polished surfaces may be protected from humidity by applying antireflection coating layers"). Direct UV-stage gas-environment dependence is not measured.
- `KD-UV280-011`: UV-induced BBO degradation mechanism. Kubota1998 supplies the empirical `p(E) = C exp(-K/E)` damage-probability model and the thermal-excitation → multiphoton-excitation electron-supply argument. The post-pulsed-operation HeNe tomographic image (Fig. 5) demonstrates microdefect formation along the UV propagation direction, not the green direction — direct evidence that UV drives the failure.
